What is a Pronoun?

A pronoun is a word that takes the place of a noun, so we don't have to repeat the same name again and again. Pronouns are like understudies in a play — they jump in and do the job of a noun! In this lesson, we will explore three important types of pronouns: Subject, Object, and Possessive pronouns.

Look at this example. Without pronouns, sentences sound repetitive and silly:

😵 Without a pronoun:

"Maya loves dogs. Maya has two dogs. The dogs love Maya, and the dogs belong to Maya."

😄 With pronouns — much better!

Maya (x4) ➜ She / her / hers

"Maya loves dogs. She has two dogs. The dogs love her, and the dogs are hers." 🐶

👀 Notice: "She" replaced the subject, "her" replaced the object, and "hers" showed ownership — that's all three pronoun types working together!

🌈 Lesson 1

Subject Pronouns

Let's explore the pronoun that starts the action in a sentence — the Subject Pronoun!

🙋
Type 1 of 3

What is a Subject Pronoun?

📖 A Subject Pronoun is a pronoun that takes the place of the noun doing the action in a sentence. It always comes before the verb and tells us who or what is performing the action.

📋 The Complete List of Subject Pronouns

IYouHe SheItWeThey

✏️ Rules to Remember

  • A subject pronoun always comes at the beginning of the sentence or clause, right before the verb.
  • Use "I" when talking about yourself, and always write it with a capital letter — even in the middle of a sentence!
  • Use "He" for one boy/man, "She" for one girl/woman, and "It" for one animal, object, or idea.
  • Use "We" when you and others are doing the action together, and "They" for two or more people, animals, or things.
  • A subject pronoun replaces the whole noun or noun phrase that is doing the action — not just part of it.

💬 Example Sentences

I read a book every night.
(I = the person doing the reading)
She sings beautifully.
(She = replaces a girl's name, e.g. "Anna")
It is raining outside.
(It = used for weather/objects/animals)
They are playing soccer in the park.
(They = replaces "the boys and girls")
We are going to the zoo tomorrow.
(We = the speaker + others)
He finished his homework early.
(He = replaces a boy's name, e.g. "Marco")

⚠️ Common Mistake to Avoid

❌ "Her went to school."
✅ "She went to school." (use a subject pronoun before the verb, not an object pronoun!)
🌈 Lesson 2

Object Pronouns

Now let's meet the pronoun that receives the action — the Object Pronoun!

🎯
Type 2 of 3

What is an Object Pronoun?

📖 An Object Pronoun is a pronoun that takes the place of the noun receiving the action. It usually comes after the verb, or after a small word called a preposition, like "to," "for," "with," or "at."

📋 The Complete List of Object Pronouns

meyouhim heritusthem

✏️ Rules to Remember

  • An object pronoun comes after the verb (the action word) — it answers the question "to whom?" or "what?"
  • It can also follow a preposition, such as: to me, for her, with them, at us, near it.
  • Notice that "you" and "it" look the same in both subject and object form — only their position in the sentence changes their job!
  • Never use an object pronoun as the subject of a sentence (it cannot do the action — it can only receive it).
  • A quick test: if you can say "to ___" or "for ___" before the pronoun and it sounds correct, it's probably an object pronoun.

💬 Example Sentences

Mom gave the cookies to them.
(them = receives the action "gave to")
The teacher called me to the front.
(me = receives the action "called")
Please sit next to her.
(her = follows the preposition "next to")
Can you help us with this puzzle?
(us = receives the action "help")
I saw him at the library.
(him = receives the action "saw")
The cat is hiding — I can't find it.
(it = receives the action "find")

⚠️ Common Mistake to Avoid

❌ "Give the ball to I."
✅ "Give the ball to me." (use an object pronoun after a preposition, not a subject pronoun!)
🌈 Lesson 3

Possessive Pronouns

Finally, let's learn the pronoun that shows who owns something — the Possessive Pronoun!

🔑
Type 3 of 3

What is a Possessive Pronoun?

📖 A Possessive Pronoun is a pronoun that shows ownership — it tells us that something belongs to someone. It replaces a noun + 's (like "Maya's") completely, and it stands alone in the sentence.

📋 The Complete List of Possessive Pronouns

mineyourshis hersitsourstheirs

✏️ Rules to Remember

  • A possessive pronoun replaces a noun completely — it does NOT come before another noun (different from "my," "your," "her," which are possessive adjectives that DO come before a noun).
  • Compare: "This is my book" (possessive adjective + noun) vs. "This book is mine" (possessive pronoun standing alone).
  • Possessive pronouns are never written with an apostrophe ('). "It's" with an apostrophe means "it is," not "belonging to it" — that word is "its."
  • Use "his" for something belonging to one boy/man, "hers" for one girl/woman, and "theirs" for two or more owners.
  • Use "ours" when something belongs to you and other people together.

💬 Example Sentences

That blue backpack is mine.
(mine = belongs to me)
Is this pencil case yours?
(yours = belongs to you)
The red bicycle is his.
(his = belongs to a boy)
This trophy is hers — she won first place!
(hers = belongs to a girl)
The dog wagged its tail happily.
(its = belongs to "it," no apostrophe)
That big house at the corner is theirs.
(theirs = belongs to more than one owner)

⚠️ Common Mistake to Avoid

❌ "This book is mine's." / "The cat licked it's paw."
✅ "This book is mine." / "The cat licked its paw." (possessive pronouns never use an apostrophe!)
💡

Quick Memory Trick!

Subject pronouns DO the action (He kicks the ball) → come BEFORE the verb. Object pronouns RECEIVE the action (The ball hit him) → come AFTER the verb. Possessive pronouns show OWNERSHIP (The ball is his) → stand alone, no apostrophe!
