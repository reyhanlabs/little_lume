🔗 What is Present Perfect Tense? Core Concept

Definition

The Present Perfect Tense is used to talk about something that happened in the past but is still connected to the present moment. It links a past action or experience to "now".

Think of it as a bridge between the past and the present — it shows that something has an effect, result, or meaning right now!

Visualizing Time

📍 An Action Connected to Now

has happened
Past
(action happened)
Now

Example: "I have finished my homework."
The action happened in the past, but the result is true right now — my homework is done!
Simple Past vs. Present Perfect

Why We Need Two Different Tenses

🔹 Simple Past

  • I watched that movie yesterday.
  • She visited Bali last year.
  • We cleaned the house on Monday.

Tells us exactly when it happened — finished and done, with a specific time.

🔸 Present Perfect

  • I have watched that movie.
  • She has visited Bali.
  • We have cleaned the house.

No specific time — focuses on the result or experience now, not when it happened.

🦊
BunRey Fox says: Present Perfect uses two helper words: have and has. We use words like already, just, yet, ever, and never with this tense — but NOT exact times like "yesterday" or "last week"!
🔨 Form & Formula How to Build It

The Magic Formula

Present Perfect Tense always uses have or has + the past participle (V3) of the verb. The trick is choosing the right helper word for the subject!

Formula

📐 Present Perfect Formula

Subject + have / has + Past Participle (V3)
Example: She + has + eaten = "She has eaten."

💡
Easy tip: Regular verbs add -ed for both V2 and V3 (clean → cleaned → cleaned). Irregular verbs change their spelling and must be memorized (eat → ate → eaten).

Remember! Always match the helper word to the subject.
✅ He has eaten. They have eaten.
❌ He have eaten. They has eaten.
⏰ When Do We Use It? Real-life Situations

3 Common Situations

We use Present Perfect Tense to show:
1️⃣ Something that just finished, with a result we can see now
2️⃣ A life experience — something you have done at some point (no specific time)
3️⃣ Something that hasn't happened yet, but might happen soon

Situation 1

✅ Recently Completed Action (Result Now)

Example SentenceResult / Meaning Now
I have just finished my homework.My homework is done — I can play now!
She has already cleaned her room.Her room is clean now.
They have just arrived.They are here now!
Situation 2

🌍 Life Experience (No Specific Time)

Example SentenceMeaning
I have visited Yogyakarta.At some point in my life, I went there. (when isn't important)
Have you ever ridden a horse?Asking about your life experience.
She has never tried durian.Not once in her life, until now.
Situation 3

⏳ Not Yet Happened

Example SentenceMeaning
I haven't finished my drawing yet.Still not done, but might finish soon.
Have you done your homework yet?Asking if it's done by now.
She hasn't called me yet.Up to now, no call has come.
🔑
Key signal words for Present Perfect:
already · just · yet · ever · never · since · for

🚫 We do NOT use Present Perfect with exact past times like yesterday, last week, in 2020, two days ago.
❌ Negative & ❓ Question Forms

Making Negative & Question Sentences

To make a sentence negative, add not after "have/has" → haven't / hasn't.
To make a question, simply move have/has to the beginning of the sentence!

Negative Form

❌ Subject + haven't / hasn't + V3

PositiveNegative
I have finished my homework.I haven't finished my homework yet.
She has eaten breakfast.She hasn't eaten breakfast yet.
They have arrived.They haven't arrived yet.
Question Form

❓ Have / Has + Subject + V3 + ... ?

StatementQuestionPossible Answer
You have finished homework.Have you finished your homework?Yes, I have. / No, I haven't.
She has visited Bali.Has she visited Bali?Yes, she has. / No, she hasn't.
They have eaten lunch.Have they eaten lunch?Yes, they have. / No, they haven't.

💬 Dialogue Practice Real Conversations
Dialogue 1 – Finished Homework?

Have You Done It Yet? 📚

👩‍🏫
Teacher
Have you finished your English homework yet, Dani?
→ Question form: Have + subject + V3 ... + yet?
👦
Dani
Yes, I have! I have already finished it this morning.
→ "have already finished" = the task is done, with a result now.
👩‍🏫
Teacher
Great! How about you, Sari? Has she finished hers too?
👧
Sari
I'm sorry, Ma'am. I haven't finished mine yet. I will finish it at home.
→ "haven't finished yet" = not done up to now, but might be done soon.
Dialogue 2 – Life Experiences

Have You Ever...? 🌍

👧
Wulan
Have you ever been to a zoo, Rafi?
→ "ever" asks about life experience, no specific time needed.
👦
Rafi
Yes, I have! I have visited Ragunan Zoo many times. I have even seen a real tiger!
→ "have visited" and "have seen" describe past experiences connected to now (he can talk about it today).
👧
Wulan
Wow! I have never seen a real tiger before. I would love to go there!
→ "have never seen" = not even once in her life so far.
🦊
